Tide Tracker - Overview

August 8, 2024

Introduction and Motivation

I designed and assembled two solar-powered tide trackers. I had roughly one month to complete the project from its conceit in late November of 2023 to its completion in early January of 2024. The project was primarily written in Python, and the plots were created using the Matplotlib and SciPy packages. The tide tracker device plots the tide height vs. time at a given location and displays the plot on an e-ink display. The data is publicly available online from the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA).

The first tide tracker was a gift for a friend who lives directly on the water, and the second was a gift for a family member who likes to go sailing on a nearby river. In the gif below, you can see the graph undergoing an update, which occurs every two hours. Notice how after the update, the black bar on the graph progresses further along to the right. The black bar indicates the section of the graph corresponding to "now."

The code for the project can be found at https://github.com/briansgithub/TideTracker_repo

Design Rationale

I decided to make two tide trackers for several reasons:

  1. The two recipients live near different rivers.
  2. The picture frames ship from Amazon as a four-pack at a low unit price. In order to fit all of the components into one enclosure, I had to stack and glue two frames on top of each other. Two of the frames match the lighter, sandy color scheme of my friend's house, and the other two match the darker, rustic color scheme of my family member's house. Perfect.
  3. Nearly 80% of the total time and effort for the project went into designing the physical layout and writing the code. These are one-time overhead costs, so the development work from the first unit can simply be used for free on all subsequent units (except for the cost of the parts and the time/energy required for assembly).

Constraints

  1. Monetary cost: This constraint usually applies to most projects.
  2. Aesthetics: The display had to be large enough to be readable and stand out, but not so large to be prohibitively expensive. I also had to make a suitable frame to encase all of the electronics. The decision was also made to use a black-and-white-only e-ink display over a multicolored e-ink display for cost and simplicity.
  3. Solar powered: It only seemed natural for a device that tracks the tides to be solar powered. This also gives the project a unique and challenging constraint. Being a low-power device also means that it could be battery powered and therefore portable! Even after a few cloudy days, I still want the tide tracker to be able to run on its own for a few days, which affects the selected battery capacity.
  4. Time frame: The inception for the idea was September of 2023. Ideally, I could just recreate an open-source project entirely, but I was not fully satisfied with what I saw, so I decided to make mine from scratch. The deadline was approximately one month later on Christmas of 2023.

Description

The plot below displays one snapshot of the tides in Fort Myers, FL on January 5, 2024.

Chart Elements:

  • The y-axis represents the tide height*, and the x-axis represents time.
  • The black bar along the curve of the plot represents the current section of the graph corresponding to "now."**
  • The light regions represent the daytime and the dark shaded regions represent the nighttime.
  • When the present time passes midnight and transitions into tomorrow, the entire graph shifts to the left so that the center of the screen always focuses on noon of "today," the left side of the screen always extends 12 hours into "yesterday," and the right side always extends 12 hours into "tomorrow."
  • Sunrise and sunset are displayed both in the upper-right corner of the screen as well as directly on the plot.
  • High tide and low tide times are displayed nearby to the corresponding peaks and valleys on the graph.

*height: The values on the graph's y-axis represent the height of the water relative to the MLLW (Mean Lower Low Water) of the geographic region, which is the standard water level reference point used for maritime navigation.

**"now": Due to the low refresh rate (updates every 2 hours to save power), "now" corresponds to a sweep along the curve corresponding to the interval of the refresh period rather than a single point.

Power

The tide tracker contains a 4500mAH LiPo battery, and therefore can run on its own for several days. To charge the device, there is a USB-C port and five JST connectors for connecting solar panels. Either can be used to charge the device. For the second tide tracker, I created an extension cable for the solar panels' JST connectors so that the device could stay inside while the solar panels could go outside.

I also designed and resin printed custom stands for the solar panels.

Extensibility: Varying the Location or Network

What if my friend moves to a new address or changes their Wi-Fi network? I included a webpage hosted on the device for configuring the settings. If the device becomes disconnected from the Wi-Fi network then the error image below appears on the screen.

The user can toggle a switch to boot the device into "Setup" mode, where the Raspberry Pi Zero W broadcasts a Wi-Fi hotspot.

On a mobile device, the Wi-Fi network named "tide-tracker" will then be visible. Connecting to it opens the configuration page.
